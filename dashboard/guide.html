1. The big idea

A strategy is just a rule that answers one question every day: should I be holding this stock, or sitting in cash? The system applies five such rules to price history and shows you three things: how each rule did in the past (Backtests), what each rule signals today across ~40 stocks (Opportunities), and the signals drawn on a price chart (Charts). Alongside, it shows a company-strength score (from fundamentals) and news sentiment to help you judge a signal.

How often the data changes

  • Live prices — about every 2 minutes during market hours.
  • News & Opportunities — hourly, 7am–9pm ET on weekdays.
  • Backtests, fundamentals, charts — once daily (4:30pm ET) and Saturday morning.

2. How to read a price chart

Close (white line): the stock's actual closing price each day — what you would pay.

SMA20 (blue) & SMA50 (orange): "Simple Moving Averages." SMA20 is the average of the last 20 closing prices; SMA50 the last 50. They smooth out daily noise so you can see the trend. Example: if AAPL's last 20 closes average $290 and its last 50 average $270, the short-term average ($290) is above the long-term ($270) — that's an uptrend.

Bollinger Bands (green dashed): SMA20 plus/minus 2 standard deviations. They measure how far price has stretched from its average. Near the lower band = unusually cheap (oversold); near the upper band = unusually expensive (overbought). Bands widen in volatile times, squeeze when calm.

Green ▲ / red ▼: where the selected strategy would have bought and sold.

Reading the trend: price above both averages with SMA20 over SMA50 and both rising = bullish. The reverse = bearish. Price hugging the upper band = strong momentum (but risky to chase); price at the lower band in an otherwise healthy stock = a possible bounce.

3. The five strategies — with examples

Momentum (trend-following)

Rule: hold when SMA20 > SMA50, unless RSI > 75 (overheated). Example: SMA20 crosses above SMA50 on MSFT → buy; it stays long until SMA20 drops back below SMA50. Good: steady, sustained trends. Bad: choppy markets (it gets whipsawed in and out), and it's late to react at turning points.

Breakout (trend-following, Donchian channel)

Rule: buy when price closes above its highest price of the last 20 days; sell when it closes below the lowest of the last 10 days. Example: NVDA's prior 20-day high was $130; it closes at $131 → breakout buy. Good: catches big sustained moves and cuts losers fast (low drawdowns). Bad: "false breakouts" in sideways markets — it buys the pop, then price falls back.

Mean-reversion (counter-trend, RSI)

Rule: buy when RSI < 30 (oversold); sell when RSI > 50 (recovered). Example: a stock drops hard, RSI hits 28 → buy expecting a bounce; sell when RSI climbs back to 50. Good: range-bound markets and rebounds (it protected capital in the 2022 downturn). Bad: strong downtrends — buying an "oversold" stock that keeps falling is "catching a falling knife."

MACD (trend/momentum)

Rule: hold when the MACD line (12-day EMA minus 26-day EMA) is above its 9-day signal line. EMA = Exponential Moving Average, a moving average that weights recent days more. Good: trends, and it reacts faster than the SMA crossover. Bad: flip-flops in choppy markets.

Bollinger (counter-trend, volatility)

Rule: buy when price closes below the lower band (statistically cheap); sell when it returns to the middle (SMA20). Good: volatile, mean-reverting names. Bad: strong trends — in a powerful move, price can ride a band for weeks.

Two families: trend-followers (momentum, breakout, MACD) ride moves and sit out downtrends — they tend to win big in bull runs and protect in bears, but give up some upside. Mean-reverters (RSI, Bollinger) buy weakness and sell into strength — great in choppy markets, dangerous in real downtrends.

4. Technical terms — defined with examples

  • Moving Average (SMA/EMA): the average price over N days, recalculated daily. Smooths noise to reveal trend. EMA reacts faster (weights recent days).
  • RSI (Relative Strength Index): a 0–100 gauge of recent up- vs down-moves. Below 30 = oversold (beaten down); above 70 = overbought (stretched). Example: RSI 25 means heavy recent selling — possible bounce.
  • MACD: difference between a fast and slow EMA; crossing its signal line flags momentum turning up or down.
  • Bollinger Bands / Standard deviation: std-dev measures how spread out prices are (volatility). Wide bands = jumpy stock; narrow = calm.
  • Donchian channel: the highest high and lowest low over a lookback window — the basis of the breakout rule.
  • Overbought / Oversold: stretched far above / below normal — prone to a pullback / bounce, though not guaranteed.
  • Momentum vs Mean-reversion: momentum bets that what's rising keeps rising; mean-reversion bets that extremes snap back to average.

5. The dashboard metrics — what good looks like

  • Total return: overall % gain/loss over the period. +50% means $100 became $150.
  • CAGR: return expressed per year. +20% CAGR ≈ doubling every ~3.6 years.
  • Max drawdown: worst peak-to-trough fall — the "how much pain" number. −40% means at the worst point you were down 40% from the high. Smaller (closer to 0) is better.
  • Sharpe ratio: return per unit of risk. Above 1 = good; above 2 = excellent; below 0 = lost money risk-adjusted. This matters more than raw return — it rewards smooth gains over wild swings.
  • Trades: number of buy/sell round-trips. More trades = more commissions and slippage eating returns.
  • Time in market: % of days actually holding vs in cash. Low % with decent returns = efficient (and you were exposed to less risk).
  • Buy & Hold: the benchmark — what you'd get just buying and never selling. A strategy earns its keep only if it beats this on a risk-adjusted basis or cuts drawdown meaningfully.

6. Company strength (fundamentals) — why it matters

Technical signals say when; fundamentals say whether the business is any good. Buying signals on financially strong companies is generally safer than on weak ones.

  • Strength score (0–100): our blended quality score. ≥65 = solid; <40 = weak.
  • Profit margin: profit kept per dollar of sales. 25% means $0.25 profit per $1 of revenue. Higher = more efficient.
  • ROE (Return on Equity): profit generated per dollar of shareholder money. Higher = better use of capital (15%+ is strong).
  • Revenue growth: how fast sales are rising year over year. Positive and accelerating is good.
  • Debt-to-equity: how much the company borrows vs its own capital. Lower = safer; very high debt is risky in downturns.
  • P/E (Price-to-Earnings): price divided by annual earnings per share — how many years of earnings you're paying for. P/E 30 = paying 30× earnings. High P/E = priced for growth (riskier if growth slows); low P/E = cheaper but maybe for a reason.

7. News & sentiment

The News page pulls market headlines; each is scored for sentiment from −1 (very negative) to +1 (very positive) using keywords. On Opportunities, "News mood" is the average for that stock. Use it as context — a buy signal with strongly negative news deserves a second look. (Note: news doesn't auto-trigger trades here, and no feed shows news before it's public.)

8. Using each page

  • Opportunities: today's signals. Tick "Fresh buys only" + "Strong companies only" for a high-conviction shortlist; click a row for a chart + plain-English read.
  • Charts: pick a ticker, choose a strategy, and see exactly where it bought/sold, with the explainer panel on the right.
  • Backtests: confirm a strategy's historical behavior on that stock — especially the 2022 bear column for how it handles downturns.
  • Overview: live prices + the strategy leaderboard (which rule is strongest risk-adjusted).

9. Honest reminders

  • These are mechanical rules, not predictions. No strategy guarantees profit.
  • Prefer high Sharpe + low drawdown over the flashiest return.
  • Backtests ignore fees, slippage, and timing — real results are worse. Always paper-trade forward first.
  • Spread risk across strategies and stocks, size positions small, and decide your exit before you enter.
